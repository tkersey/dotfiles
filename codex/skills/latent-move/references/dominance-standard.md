# Dominance Standard

A move is dominant only if it materially beats plausible alternatives on at least one major axis while not losing badly on the others.

## Major axes

- capability expansion
- future-work compression
- invariant strengthening
- proof or measurement value
- interface simplification
- reversibility and diff discipline
- implementation risk concentration
- time to first trustworthy proof
- blast-radius reduction
- decision unblock value

## Gates

Eliminate or heavily penalize candidates that fail these gates.

### Evidence gate

The payoff must be grounded in current artifact state.

### Proof gate

The move must have a credible first proof signal.

### Diff gate

The change surface must be bounded relative to payoff.

### Theater gate

Ambition must improve leverage, proof, optionality, invariants, or capability. Ambition alone is not value.

### Constraint gate

The move must not violate hard constraints or rely on a decision not yet made.

### Reversibility gate

A hard-to-unwind move needs commensurate payoff and stronger proof.

## Ranking pressure

Prefer moves that produce learning even when they fail, create reusable artifacts, make future validation easier, simplify interfaces or invariants, lower risk concentration, expose hidden constraints cheaply, and have a narrow minimum viable diff.

Penalize moves that are mostly aesthetic, require broad churn, require speculative architecture, defer proof too long, move risk into hidden state, create lock-in before evidence, or depend on unstated product decisions.

## Verdicts

### Winner

Use only when one move clearly dominates and has a credible first proof signal.

### No dominant move

Use when comparable candidates remain tied or trade off along different axes without a clear material winner. Name the fastest discriminating check.

### Insufficient evidence

Use when a missing fact blocks fair comparison. Name the missing evidence, why it matters, and how each likely outcome would route.
